  • Wave slides

    A series of slides where we play with sine waves

    by Gabriel Lyu

  • sin() is a wave

    sin(a) is the y coordinate of a point on a circle when it's connected to the centre and forms an angle of 'a', right?

    Right. And as 'a' increases, you see a wave.

  • sin() has ...

    ... an amplitude and a phase angle

    Its amplitude is 1. Its phase angle increases at a constant pace.

    Can we make some changes?

  • Changing amplitude

    Can we make its amplitude change like a sine function?

    Yes, we can.

    That's called amplitude modulation.

  • Changing frequency

    If phase angle is a distance that the point travels, then frequency is its speed.

    Do we have to walk at a constant speed?

    Of course not. Walk at a speed of sine function, if you like.

  • Adding sine functions?

    That's when things get fun.

    It can look like a small sine wave riding on a bigger sine wave.

    But it can also look like...

  • ... a square wave

    By cleverly choosing amplitudes and frequencies, you can get a wave of surprising shapes.

  • ... or a sawtooth wave

    But how do we know what amplitudes and frequencies we want?

    We do something called Fourier Series expansion.

    Or, we look at a signal from frequency perspective. That's called the signal's ...

  • ... frequency spectrum!

    Have a play with the panel. You can change the amplitude of each frequency.

  • Welcome back, circle

    Remember that point with a y coordinate as sin(a)? Actually its x coordinate is cos(a).

    And did I mention that cos(a) is just sin(a + 90 degrees)?

    Great, we've got a pair of sine waves now.

  • We can change their amplitudes

    Like squeezing a ball.

  • Or their frequencies

    Roar! What is this?

    This is called Lissajous figure.
